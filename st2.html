ST2 WPBA Requirements

Based on RCGP WPBA Requirements. Minimum requirements per 6-month placement:

  • ‚úì Case-Based Discussions (CBDs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    Should demonstrate increasing complexity and independent decision-making.

  • ‚úì Clinical Evaluation Exercises (CEXs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    Observed clinical encounters with structured feedback. In ST3, COTs replace CEXs.

  • ‚úì Multi-Source Feedback (MSF): 1 per year

    Minimum 8 responses from colleagues across different roles.

  • ‚úì Reflective Entries: 18 per 6-month placement

    Part of 36 reflections per year total. Should link to 3+ competencies, demonstrate learning. These serve as your learning log.

  • ‚úì Capabilities: Must be referenced and completed in full for each 6-month block

    All capabilities need to be demonstrated and documented within each placement period.

  • ‚úì Domains of Experience: Complete where possible

    Unlike capabilities, domains don't need to be complete for each block, but aim to cover them throughout training.

  • ‚úì Personal Development Plan (PDP): Updated and reviewed with ES

    Should be updated regularly, show progression, identify learning needs.

  • ‚úì Interim Educational Supervisor Review (ESR): In first 6 months

    Mid-placement review with your Educational Supervisor to assess progress.

  • ‚úì Clinical Supervisor Report (CSR): From each placement

    Required from every clinical supervisor at end of placement.

  • ‚úì End of Year Review: At end of each ST year

    Comprehensive review of your progress through the training year (may be adjusted for trainees out of sync/LTFT).

üìù Remember: You need 36 reflections per year total, which means 18 reflections per 6-month placement.

üéØ AKT Preparation (ST2 Focus)

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) is one of the three components of the MRCGP exam. It's a 160-question, 2 hour 40 minute computer-based exam that tests your clinical knowledge, evidence-based practice, and understanding of primary care organisation and management.

When to Take AKT

Most trainees take AKT in ST2 or early ST3. Plan for 3 months of dedicated study. The exam is held multiple times per year, so you can choose a date that fits your training schedule.

What the AKT Tests

Clinical Medicine (~80%)

128 questions covering all major systems and common presentations in primary care.

Evidence-Based Practice (~10%)

16 questions on interpreting evidence, statistics, and research methodology.

Primary Care Organisation (~10%)

16 questions on practice management, QI, and healthcare systems.

Study Strategy

Start early ‚Äî begin 3-4 months before your planned exam date. Use question banks to practice AKT-style questions regularly. Focus on guidelines ‚Äî NICE, CKS, and BNF are essential. Cover all major systems systematically, and practice under timed conditions with mock exams.

The key is consistent practice. Don't just read ‚Äî actively answer questions, understand why answers are correct, and identify your knowledge gaps. Use your clinical cases to reinforce learning, and link your AKT study to your portfolio reflections.

Key Topics to Cover

Cardiovascular (hypertension, heart failure, AF), Respiratory (asthma, COPD, infections), Diabetes and endocrine, Mental health (depression, anxiety, dementia), Women's health (contraception, menopause), Child health (common presentations), and EBP & PCOM (evidence-based practice, patient-centred care).

GPST2: Timeline (Integration & Pressure)

Everyone is on different timelines ‚Äî this is a conceptual guide. The emotional and developmental pattern is remarkably consistent, but your specific journey will be unique.

Early ST2: Slower, and worried about it

If you start GP placements here, you may suddenly feel incompetent again. Ten minutes feels impossible. Your brain feels full.

This does not mean you're bad at GP. It means you're learning a new skill set.

What helps

Use a clear consultation structure every time. Say out loud to your supervisor:

"I'm safe, but slow ‚Äî can we work on that together?"

That is a very reasonable request.

Mid ST2: The quiet dip

This is where motivation often drops. You might notice: irritability, doubting your ability, avoiding portfolio or feedback.

This is a warning phase, not a failure phase.

Do not push through silently. This is when to speak to your ES and, if needed, your TPD ‚Äî not because you're in trouble, but because support works best here.

Late ST2: Finding your rhythm

By this stage, you should be feeling more confident in your consultations and decision-making. You're starting to see the connections between hospital medicine and general practice more clearly.

What to focus on now

This is a good time to start thinking about AKT preparation if you haven't already. You should also be demonstrating increasing complexity in your cases and showing clear progression in your portfolio.

If you're still feeling stuck or uncertain, don't wait ‚Äî speak to your ES. Late ST2 is a crucial time to address any concerns before moving into ST3.

Strategic Focus Areas

1. Increased Complexity

Choose cases with multiple differentials. Demonstrate independent decision-making. Show management of uncertainty. Handle more complex multi-morbidity.

2. Portfolio Progression

Show clear improvement from ST1. Demonstrate deeper reflection. Link to more advanced competencies. Show leadership and teaching activities.

3. Exam Preparation

Balance portfolio work with AKT study. Document AKT study in learning log. Use clinical cases to reinforce learning. Plan exam date around placements.
