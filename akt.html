Applied Knowledge Test - Complete Study Guide

What is the AKT?

The Applied Knowledge Test (AKT) is a computer-based exam that tests your knowledge of evidence-based medicine, clinical guidelines, and primary care practice. It's one of the two exams required for MRCGP.

Updated Format (October 2025)

  • Total questions: 160 (reduced from 200)
  • Duration: 3 hours
  • Question types: Single Best Answer (SBA) questions
  • Content: Clinical Medicine, Evidence-Based Practice (EBP), and Patient-Centred Care (PCOM)
  • Pass mark: Approximately 70% (varies by sitting)

Exam Structure

Question Distribution

  • Clinical Medicine: ~80% of questions (128 questions)
  • EBP (Evidence-Based Practice): ~10% of questions (16 questions)
  • PCOM (Patient-Centred Care): ~10% of questions (16 questions)

3-Month Study Schedule

Month 1: Foundation

  • Review major clinical topics systematically
  • Focus on NICE guidelines and CKS
  • Start question bank practice (untimed)
  • Build knowledge base

Month 2: Consolidation

  • Continue question bank practice
  • Focus on weak areas identified
  • Practice timed questions
  • Review EBP and PCOM topics

Month 3: Exam Conditions

  • Full mock exams under timed conditions
  • Final review of guidelines
  • Focus on high-yield topics
  • Mental preparation and exam technique

Three Study Strategy Modes

1. Untimed Mode

Learn at your own pace. Read explanations carefully, look up guidelines, understand the reasoning. Best for early study.

2. Timed with Notes

Practice under time pressure but allow yourself to make notes. Builds speed while maintaining learning. Best for mid-study.

3. Exam Conditions

Full exam simulation - timed, no notes, no looking up answers. Best for final preparation and identifying weak areas.

IMG AKT Success Guide: Master UK GP Thinking

This is a very high-yield area. IMGs who understand these points often gain 10‚Äì15% extra marks without learning new medicine. The AKT isn't just testing medical knowledge‚Äîit's testing UK GP thinking. Here's how to bridge that gap:

üéØ 1. The UK GP Mindset: Less is More

The biggest mistake IMGs make: Over-investigating and over-referring. UK GPs manage more in primary care than many countries. If a question asks "next step" and you're thinking "refer to specialist" or "order expensive test"‚Äîstop. Think: "What can I manage here? What's the NICE guideline?"

üí° Practice Tip: For every case, ask: "Would a UK GP manage this in primary care?" If yes, that's likely the answer.

üìö 2. NICE, CKS, BNF: Your Holy Trinity

These aren't just resources‚Äîthey're the answers. Every AKT question is based on UK guidelines. If you're guessing between options, ask: "What does NICE say?" Memorize common pathways: hypertension (ABPM first), depression (watchful waiting for mild), diabetes (HbA1c targets).

üìä 3. EBP: Your Secret Weapon (If You Master It)

Many IMGs struggle with EBP‚Äîbut if you master it, you'll gain easy marks. Memorize these formulas: ARR = Control rate - Treatment rate. NNT = 1/ARR. RRR = (Control - Treatment) / Control. Practice until you can calculate in your sleep.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake: Confusing ARR (absolute) with RRR (relative). ARR is what matters clinically. NNT uses ARR, not RRR.

üí¨ 4. PCOM: Understanding UK Healthcare Values

PCOM questions test UK healthcare culture, not just medical knowledge. Key concepts: patient autonomy (patients decide), shared decision-making (not paternalistic), capacity (presume capacity unless proven otherwise), consent (informed and voluntary).

üéØ Remember: In UK primary care, the patient is the expert in their life. Your job is to provide information and support their decision‚Äînot make it for them.

‚ö° 5. Time Management: The 1.125-Minute Rule

160 questions in 3 hours = 1.125 minutes per question. If English isn't your first language, this is brutal. Practice speed reading. Don't get stuck on one question‚Äîmark it, move on, come back if time allows.

‚è±Ô∏è Strategy: First pass: answer all "easy" questions. Second pass: tackle harder ones. Don't leave any blank‚Äîguess if you must.

üö´ 6. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

‚ùå Over-investigation: UK GPs do fewer tests. Think "watchful waiting" first.
‚ùå Wrong drugs: BNF is different. Learn UK drug names and protocols.
‚ùå Over-referral: UK GPs manage more in primary care. Refer only when necessary.
‚ùå Terminology: Learn UK terms (e.g., "GP" not "family doctor", "consultant" not "attending").
