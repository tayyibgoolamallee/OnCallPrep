ST1 WPBA Requirements

Based on RCGP WPBA Requirements. Minimum requirements per 6-month placement:

  • ‚úì Case-Based Discussions (CBDs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    At least 2 from hospital placements, 2 from GP if applicable. Should demonstrate learning and reflection.

  • ‚úì Clinical Evaluation Exercises (CEXs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    Observed clinical encounters with structured feedback. In ST3, COTs replace CEXs.

  • ‚úì Multi-Source Feedback (MSF): 1 per year

    Minimum 8 responses from colleagues across different roles.

  • ‚úì Reflective Entries: 18 per 6-month placement

    Part of 36 reflections per year total. Should link to 3+ competencies, demonstrate learning. These serve as your learning log.

  • ‚úì Capabilities: Must be referenced and completed in full for each 6-month block

    All capabilities need to be demonstrated and documented within each placement period.

  • ‚úì Domains of Experience: Complete where possible

    Unlike capabilities, domains don't need to be complete for each block, but aim to cover them throughout training.

  • ‚úì Personal Development Plan (PDP): Created and reviewed with Educational Supervisor

    Should be updated regularly, show progression, identify learning needs.

  • ‚úì Interim Educational Supervisor Review (ESR): In first 6 months

    Mid-placement review with your Educational Supervisor to assess progress.

  • ‚úì Clinical Supervisor Report (CSR): From each placement

    Required from every clinical supervisor at end of placement.

  • ‚úì End of Year Review: At end of each ST year

    Comprehensive review of your progress through the training year (may be adjusted for trainees out of sync/LTFT).

üìù Remember: You need 36 reflections per year total, which means 18 reflections per 6-month placement.

GPST1: Timeline (Survive ‚Üí Understand ‚Üí Adapt)

This is approximate, because rotations differ ‚Äî but the emotional and developmental pattern is remarkably consistent.

Early ST1: "I don't know what I'm doing"

This is the hardest bit for many people. You arrive competent on paper, but suddenly: you don't know the systems, everyone else seems quicker, you're scared of missing something.

What actually matters right now

Your job is not to be slick or impressive. Your job is to be safe, curious, and visible.

That means: ask early rather than late, say "I'm not sure" out loud, learn how escalation works on that ward / that practice.

If you feel slow, anxious, or overwhelmed ‚Äî that is normal here. What helps most is regular, boring, predictable check-ins with your clinical supervisor. Don't wait until something goes wrong.

If you're thinking "I shouldn't need this much help", that's your cue to book a meeting.

Mid ST1: Things start to look familiar

You begin recognising patterns. You're still tired, but less panicked. This is where many trainees quietly struggle because expectations increase but support doesn't always follow.

What to do now

Start asking why decisions are made, not just what. Practice writing notes that explain your thinking. Use your ES meeting to say:

"I feel better than I did, but I'm still unsure if I'm progressing normally"

That sentence alone opens doors.

Late ST1: Confidence grows, gaps remain

You'll probably feel more confident ‚Äî sometimes more confident than you should. This is the phase where gentle correction matters most. You don't want to drift.

Practical advice

Invite feedback rather than waiting for it. If something didn't go well, log it early ‚Äî not defensively. Start thinking: What kind of GP am I becoming?

If you've had a rough year, this is the time to say so. Extensions and extra support are much easier to arrange here than later.

Essential terminology to help you navigate GP training:

Training Roles

CS (Clinical Supervisor):
Day-to-day supervisor in your current placement. First point for clinical issues.
ES (Educational Supervisor):
Oversees overall progression across placements. Key for portfolio and development.
TPD (Training Programme Director):
Manages training programme. Escalate concerns here if needed.

WPBA Terms

CBD (Case-Based Discussion):
Discussion of a case with supervisor, demonstrating clinical reasoning.
COT (Consultation Observation Tool):
Observed consultation with structured feedback.
MSF (Multi-Source Feedback):
360¬∞ feedback from colleagues across different roles.
PSQ (Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire):
Patient feedback on your consultations.
CSR (Clinical Supervisor Report):
End-of-placement report from your CS.

Training Terms

ARCP (Annual Review of Competence Progression):
Annual assessment of your progress through training.
CCT (Certificate of Completion of Training):
Certificate awarded on completion of GP training.
ITP (Integrated Training Post):
Bespoke specialty posts for areas where GPs fulfil particular roles or expand unique skills (e.g., ENT, dermatology, urgent care, public health).

Plan identifying learning needs and goals.
LTFT (Less Than Full Time):
Training at reduced hours (e.g., 80%, 60%).

If You're Struggling: What to Actually Do

Here's the most important practical advice: Say something earlier than feels necessary.

Not dramatically. Not emotionally. Just clearly.

"I'm worried I'm not progressing as expected and I'd like some support."

That sentence is enough.

Early Warning Signs

Dreading work consistently
Avoiding supervision
Defensive or withdrawn
Portfolio avoidance
Feedback saying the same thing repeatedly
üëâ These are signals to escalate, not hide.

How to Escalate Concerns (Safely and Properly)

Think of escalation as widening the circle, not jumping off a cliff.

Step 1: Clinical Supervisor

Talk about day-to-day safety, workload, confidence.

If you leave feeling unheard, that's information ‚Äî not failure.

Step 2: Educational Supervisor

This is the key relationship. Be honest. ESs hate surprises far more than difficulties.

Ask directly:

"Do you have concerns about my progression?"

If the answer is yes, ask:

"What support would help?"

Step 3: Training Programme Director (TPD)

TPDs are not disciplinarians. They are problem-solvers.

Involving them early often prevents ARCP issues, protects your training record, and gives you options (extra time, targeted placements, LTFT).

If you feel "too embarrassed" to speak to a TPD, that's often a sign you should.

Step 4: Wider support

Use these alongside training:

  • Your own GP
  • Occupational Health
  • BMA (especially if processes feel unfair)
  • GP Support Line

This is not weakness ‚Äî it's maintenance.

One Last Thing I Want You to Hear

Most GP trainees who struggle are not unsafe, lazy, or unsuitable. They are conscientious, tired, and holding too much in their heads.

Needing help during GP training does not predict being a poor GP ‚Äî it often predicts being a careful, reflective, humane one. If this sounds like you, that's not a bad sign. It's usually the opposite.

Strategic Focus Areas

1. Building Foundation Knowledge

Understand common presentations in primary care. Learn consultation models and frameworks. Develop history-taking and examination skills. Understand referral pathways and local services.

2. Portfolio Quality

Start early - don't leave everything to the last minute. Write reflections soon after events while details are fresh. Link to multiple competencies (aim for 3 links per reflection). Show progression and learning, not just what happened.

3. Professional Development

Build relationships with supervisors and colleagues. Attend teaching sessions and workshops. Engage with your Educational Supervisor regularly. Document learning from all sources (clinical, courses, reading).
