ST3 WPBA Requirements

Based on RCGP WPBA Requirements. Minimum requirements per 6-month placement:

  • ‚úì Case-Based Discussions (CBDs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    Complex cases, independent decision-making, demonstrate readiness for independent practice.

  • ‚úì Consultation Observation Tools (COTs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    In ST3, COTs replace CEXs. Should demonstrate safe, efficient, patient-centred consulting at independent practice level.

  • ‚úì Multi-Source Feedback (MSF): 1 per year + 1 Leadership MSF

    Minimum 8 responses. Leadership MSF should reflect developing leadership skills.

  • ‚úì Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire (PSQ): 1 per GP placement (Required in ST3)

    Minimum 20 patient responses required. This is only required in ST3, not ST1 or ST2.

  • ‚úì Reflective Entries: 18 per 6-month placement

    Part of 36 reflections per year total. Should link to 3+ competencies, demonstrate learning. These serve as your learning log.

  • ‚úì Capabilities: Must be referenced and completed in full for each 6-month block

    All capabilities need to be demonstrated and documented within each placement period.

  • ‚úì Domains of Experience: Complete where possible

    Unlike capabilities, domains don't need to be complete for each block, but aim to cover them throughout training.

  • ‚úì Personal Development Plan (PDP): Focus on areas for ongoing development post-CCT

    Should be updated regularly, show progression, identify learning needs for post-CCT practice.

  • ‚úì Interim Educational Supervisor Review (ESR): In first 6 months

    Mid-placement review with your Educational Supervisor to assess progress.

  • ‚úì Clinical Supervisor Report (CSR): From each placement

    Required from every clinical supervisor at end of placement.

  • ‚úì End of Year Review: At end of each ST year

    Comprehensive review of your progress through the training year (may be adjusted for trainees out of sync/LTFT).

üéØ SCA Preparation (ST3 Focus)

The Simulated Consultation Assessment (SCA) is one of the three components of the MRCGP exam. It's a practical exam that tests your ability to integrate and apply clinical, professional, and communication skills in a realistic GP setting.

What the SCA Involves

You'll face 12 simulated consultations, each lasting 12 minutes. Professional role-players present realistic cases that respond to your approach, just like real patients. The exam is conducted remotely in a local GP surgery, making it as close to real practice as possible.

This isn't about memorising scripts ‚Äî it's about demonstrating how you think, communicate, and manage uncertainty in the moment. The cases may involve patients, their carers, parents, or other health and social care workers, reflecting the variety of situations you'll encounter in general practice.

The Three Marking Domains

Domain 1: Data Gathering & Interpretation

How you gather information, explore the patient's perspective, and interpret what you find.

Domain 2: Clinical Management (1.5x weighted)

Your clinical reasoning, decision-making, and management planning. This domain carries more weight.

Domain 3: Interpersonal Skills

How you relate to patients, build rapport, and communicate effectively.

Study Strategy

Usually taken in ST3, after passing AKT. Plan for 2-3 months of dedicated practice. Work through mock cases regularly ‚Äî not just reading them, but actually practicing the consultations. Practice time management ‚Äî 12 minutes goes quickly, so learn to structure your consultations efficiently.

Practice priming exercises ‚Äî these 3-minute scenarios help you prepare mentally for each case. Use structured checklists to self-assess your performance after each practice case. If possible, record yourself practicing ‚Äî seeing yourself consult is incredibly revealing.

The key is building confidence through repetition. The more you practice, the more natural the structure becomes, leaving you free to focus on the patient and the clinical reasoning.

Grading System

Each domain is scored 0-4 points. Domain 2 is weighted 1.5x, giving a total of 10.5 points. Clear Pass (>9/10.5), Pass (6-9/10.5), Fail (3-6/10.5), Clear Fail (<3/10.5).

GPST3: Timeline (Transition to Independent GP)

Everyone is on different timelines ‚Äî this is a conceptual guide. The emotional and developmental pattern is remarkably consistent, but your specific journey will be unique.

Early ST3: "Everyone thinks I should know this"

Responsibility suddenly feels real. You may feel lonely with decisions. This is normal.

Good GPST3 trainees:
  • Discuss their reasoning, not just outcomes
  • Admit uncertainty early
  • Learn how local services actually work

Mid ST3: Building independence

By this stage, you should be managing more complex cases independently and feeling more confident in your clinical decision-making. You're starting to think like an independent GP, not just a trainee.

What to focus on now

This is a crucial time for exam preparation if you haven't completed AKT or SCA. You should also be demonstrating leadership through QI projects, teaching, and taking on more responsibility in practice management.

Your portfolio should show clear progression and readiness for independent practice. If you're feeling uncertain about your progress, this is the time to address it with your ES.

Late ST3: Letting go

Confidence and anxiety often sit side by side here. You're not aiming to feel fearless ‚Äî just appropriately cautious and reflective.

If you're still struggling at this stage, escalation is not a disaster. Many strong GPs needed extra time or tailored support.

Strategic Focus Areas

1. Readiness for Independent Practice

Handle complex multi-morbidity confidently. Manage uncertainty appropriately. Demonstrate safe practice. Show awareness of limitations and when to seek help.

2. Leadership & Development

Teaching and supervision activities. Quality improvement projects. Practice management understanding. Career planning and next steps.

3. Exam Completion

Ensure AKT is passed (if not done in ST2). Plan SCA date early in ST3. Balance exam prep with portfolio work. Prepare for ARCP and CCT application.

Quality Improvement: Making a Real Difference

Quality Improvement (QI) isn't just a portfolio requirement ‚Äî it's a fundamental skill that defines excellent general practice. In ST3, you're not just learning to be a safe GP; you're learning to be a GP who actively improves the care you and your practice provide.

Why QI Matters

QI projects demonstrate leadership, commitment to improving patient care, and the ability to work systematically. They show you can identify problems, test solutions, and measure impact ‚Äî skills that are essential for independent practice and highly valued by employers.

The PDSA Cycle: A Practical Framework

The Plan-Do-Study-Act cycle is the foundation of QI. It's not about perfection ‚Äî it's about learning through small, manageable changes.

Finding Your QI Project

The best QI projects come from problems you genuinely care about. Look around your practice: what frustrates you? What could be better? What do patients complain about? What do staff struggle with?

Project Ideas That Work Well
  • Improving medication review processes and safety
  • Enhancing patient access to services (appointments, results, advice)
  • Improving chronic disease management (diabetes, hypertension, COPD)
  • Reducing DNAs (Did Not Attend) rates
  • Improving vaccination uptake
  • Enhancing patient safety measures (safeguarding, medication errors)
  • Improving communication with patients (test results, referral outcomes)
Documenting Your QI Project

Keep a clear record: the problem you identified, baseline data, your intervention, results, and what you learned. This isn't just for your portfolio ‚Äî it's for you. Being able to articulate what you did, why, and what happened demonstrates maturity, leadership, and readiness for independent practice.

Leadership & Feedback: Learning to Lead and Listen

In ST3, feedback isn't just about meeting requirements ‚Äî it's about developing the emotional intelligence and professional maturity that define excellent GPs. How you seek, receive, and act on feedback says everything about your readiness for independent practice.

Multi-Source Feedback (MSF): Seeing Yourself Through Others' Eyes

MSF is powerful because it shows you how you're perceived by the people you work with. In ST3, you need 1 standard MSF per year (minimum 8 responses) plus 1 Leadership MSF that specifically reflects your developing leadership skills.

For Your Leadership MSF, Consider Asking:
  • Clinical supervisors who have seen you teach or supervise others
  • Practice managers who have worked with you on projects
  • Practice nurses who have collaborated with you
  • Administrative staff who have experienced your communication and leadership style
  • Peers (if appropriate and relevant to your role)

Complete MSF early in your placement ‚Äî ideally within the first 2-3 months. This gives you time to reflect, create an action plan, and demonstrate growth before your end-of-placement review.

Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire (PSQ): Hearing What Matters Most

The PSQ is only required in ST3 ‚Äî this is when you're demonstrating readiness for independent practice, and patient feedback is a crucial part of that. You need 1 PSQ per GP placement with a minimum of 20 patient responses.

Patient feedback can be humbling, inspiring, and sometimes difficult to hear. But it's real. These are the people you're here to serve, and their perspective on your communication, empathy, and clinical care is invaluable.

Getting Meaningful Patient Feedback

Ask a diverse range of patients ‚Äî not just the ones you think will give positive feedback. Include patients with complex needs, those you've had difficult conversations with, and those from different backgrounds. This gives you a more honest picture of your practice.

Processing Feedback: The SHARP Model

Receiving feedback well is a skill. The SHARP model helps you process feedback constructively, even when it's difficult to hear.

S - Share

Acknowledge the feedback openly. Don't dismiss it or become defensive.

H - Hear

Listen actively. Try to understand the perspective behind the feedback.

A - Acknowledge

Thank the person giving feedback. It takes courage to give honest feedback.

R - Reflect

Think deeply about what you've heard. What's true? What can you learn?

P - Plan

Decide what you'll do differently. Create a concrete action plan.

Turning Feedback Into Growth

Thank those who provided feedback ‚Äî genuinely. Reflect on both positive and constructive feedback. Create a clear action plan for areas identified for development. Document your reflection in your portfolio, linking it to relevant competencies. Share your action plan with your supervisor and ask for their support. Most importantly, follow up on your progress ‚Äî show that you've taken the feedback seriously.

Feedback is a gift. Even when it's difficult to hear, it helps you grow. Your response to feedback ‚Äî especially difficult feedback ‚Äî demonstrates professionalism, emotional intelligence, and readiness for independent practice.

Career Planning & Life Beyond CCT

As you approach the end of ST3, you're not just finishing training ‚Äî you're beginning your career as an independent GP. This transition brings excitement, opportunity, and new challenges. ST3 is the time to think seriously about your career post-CCT.

Key Career Decisions

Practice Type

Partnership, salaried, locum, or portfolio career? Each has different benefits, responsibilities, and lifestyle implications.

Special Interests

What areas do you want to develop? Consider your clinical interests, teaching, research, or leadership aspirations.

Location

Where do you want to work? Consider family, lifestyle, opportunities, and the type of practice environment that suits you.

Further Training

Any additional qualifications or courses? Consider what will enhance your career and what you're genuinely interested in.

Understanding how GPs are set up, how practices run, how you can get involved, and how to learn from experiences like triage, medical report writing, QI, and leadership ‚Äî these skills will shape your career trajectory and job satisfaction.

One Last Thing I Want You to Hear

Most GP trainees who struggle are not unsafe, lazy, or unsuitable. They are conscientious, tired, and holding too much in their heads.

Needing help during GP training does not predict being a poor GP ‚Äî it often predicts being a careful, reflective, humane one. If this sounds like you, that's not a bad sign. It's usually the opposite.
