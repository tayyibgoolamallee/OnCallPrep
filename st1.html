GP training isn't just three years of service jobs. It's a progressive shift from hospital doctor ‚Üí community generalist, from doing tasks ‚Üí managing uncertainty, and from supervised decisions ‚Üí independent clinical judgement.

üìÖ GP Training Timeline

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note

All training deaneries are different. This is a general guide - your specific rotations, ITP timing, and hospital/GP placements will vary. Always check with your local training programme.

ST1
Foundation Year (Months 1-12)
  • Hospital rotations (typically 6-12 months)
  • May include GP placement
  • ITP (Initial Training Period) - varies by deanery
  • Focus: Building foundation, unlearning hospital reflexes
ST2
Development Year (Months 13-24)
  • Mix of hospital and GP placements
  • AKT preparation and exam
  • Focus: Integration, clinical maturity
ST3
Exit Year (Months 25-36)
  • Primarily GP placements
  • SCA preparation and exam
  • Focus: Independence, consultant-level thinking
Key Terms:
  • ITP (Initial Training Period): Early training period, structure varies by deanery
  • Hospital Years: Rotations in secondary care (A&E, paeds, psych, medicine, etc.)
  • GP Posts: Placements in general practice, typically increasing in duration through training

ST1 WPBA Requirements

Based on RCGP WPBA Requirements. Minimum requirements per 6-month placement:

  • ‚úì Case-Based Discussions (CBDs): Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    At least 2 from hospital placements, 2 from GP if applicable. Should demonstrate learning and reflection.

  • ‚úì Consultation Observation Tools (COTs): Minimum 2 if in GP placement

    Observed consultations with feedback from supervisor.

  • ‚úì Multi-Source Feedback (MSF): 1 per year

    Minimum 8 responses from colleagues across different roles.

  • ‚úì Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire (PSQ): 1 if in GP placement

    Minimum 20 patient responses required.

  • ‚úì Reflective Entries: Minimum 4 per 6-month placement

    Part of 36 reflections per year total. Should link to 3+ competencies, demonstrate learning.

  • ‚úì Learning Log: Regular entries documenting learning activities

    Document all learning: clinical cases, courses, reading, teaching sessions.

  • ‚úì Personal Development Plan (PDP): Created and reviewed with Educational Supervisor

    Should be updated regularly, show progression, identify learning needs.

  • ‚úì Clinical Supervisor Report (CSR): From each placement

    Required from every clinical supervisor at end of placement.

üìù Remember: You need 36 reflections per year total, typically split over 2-3 rotations (4-6 months each). This means approximately 12-18 reflections per rotation depending on your training structure.

üìÜ GPST1: Month-by-Month (Survive ‚Üí Understand ‚Üí Adapt)

This is approximate, because rotations differ ‚Äî but the emotional and developmental pattern is remarkably consistent.

1-3 Months 1-3: Shock & Survival

Very Common Feelings:
  • "Everyone else knows more than me"
  • Fear of making mistakes
  • Over-reliance on seniors
What to Focus On:
  • Patient safety > confidence
  • Learn local systems (referrals, IT, escalation)
  • Start portfolio early (little and often)
If You're Struggling:

This is expected. Book early meetings with:

  • Clinical Supervisor (CS)
  • Educational Supervisor (ES)

‚ö†Ô∏è Red flag: avoiding asking for help

4-6 Months 4-6: Pattern Recognition Begins

What Changes:
  • Common presentations feel familiar
  • You start anticipating plans
What to Work On:
  • Clear documentation
  • SBAR handovers
  • Reflective learning logs (quality > quantity)
Common Trap: Comparing yourself to confident peers

7-9 Months 7-9: Confidence vs Competence Gap

You may feel confident ‚Äî but still need supervision.

Focus:
  • Know your limits
  • Ask "what's the worst-case scenario?"
  • Start exam awareness (not intense prep)

10-12 Months 10-12: Preparation for GP Thinking

Shift:
  • Less "what's the diagnosis?"
  • More "what's the risk if I miss something?"
Do Now:
  • Identify learning needs for GP placements
  • Be honest in supervisor reports

üìñ Glossary: Getting Your Bearings

Essential terminology to help you navigate GP training:

Training Roles

CS (Clinical Supervisor):
Day-to-day supervisor in your current placement. First point for clinical issues.
ES (Educational Supervisor):
Oversees overall progression across placements. Key for portfolio and development.
TPD (Training Programme Director):
Manages training programme. Escalate concerns here if needed.

WPBA Terms

CBD (Case-Based Discussion):
Discussion of a case with supervisor, demonstrating clinical reasoning.
COT (Consultation Observation Tool):
Observed consultation with structured feedback.
MSF (Multi-Source Feedback):
360¬∞ feedback from colleagues across different roles.
PSQ (Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire):
Patient feedback on your consultations.
CSR (Clinical Supervisor Report):
End-of-placement report from your CS.

Training Terms

ARCP (Annual Review of Competence Progression):
Annual assessment of your progress through training.
CCT (Certificate of Completion of Training):
Certificate awarded on completion of GP training.
ITP (Initial Training Period):
Early training period, structure varies by deanery.
PDP (Personal Development Plan):
Plan identifying learning needs and goals.
LTFT (Less Than Full Time):
Training at reduced hours (e.g., 80%, 60%).

üí° One Crucial Truth

The system works best when you speak early. Silence is what escalates risk.

Good trainees:

  • Reflect honestly
  • Ask for help
  • Escalate before crisis

Strategic Focus Areas

1. Building Foundation Knowledge

  • Understand common presentations in primary care
  • Learn consultation models and frameworks
  • Develop history-taking and examination skills
  • Understand referral pathways and local services

2. Portfolio Quality

  • Start early - don't leave everything to the last minute
  • Write reflections soon after events while details are fresh
  • Link to multiple competencies (aim for 3 links per reflection)
  • Show progression and learning, not just what happened

3. Professional Development

  • Build relationships with supervisors and colleagues
  • Attend teaching sessions and workshops
  • Engage with your Educational Supervisor regularly
  • Document learning from all sources (clinical, courses, reading)
