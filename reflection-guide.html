üîó The Three-Link Rule

Key Principle

Every reflection should link to at least three competencies. This demonstrates holistic thinking and integration of multiple skills. According to HEE guidance, this shows you understand the complexity of general practice.

Why Three Links?

üß©
Holistic Understanding

Shows you see the whole patient, not just the problem

üîÑ
Skill Integration

Demonstrates how different competencies work together

üìä
Higher-Level Reflection

Moves beyond description to analysis and learning

Example: Three-Link Reflection

‚úÖ Good Example

Case: Managing a 45-year-old man with chest pain

Three Competency Links:

  • Clinical Knowledge & Skills: Differential diagnosis of chest pain (cardiac vs non-cardiac), appropriate use of investigations
  • Communication & Partnership: Explaining risk and management options clearly, addressing patient's anxiety, shared decision-making
  • Patient Safety & Quality of Care: Appropriate investigation and safety netting, recognizing when to refer urgently

Why this works: Shows you integrated clinical knowledge, communication skills, and safety awareness - demonstrating holistic patient care.

‚ùå Poor Example

Case: Managing a 45-year-old man with chest pain

Single Competency Link:

  • Clinical Knowledge: Diagnosed chest pain

Why this doesn't work: Only links to one competency. Doesn't show integration of skills or holistic thinking.

üìã Reflection Structure

Based on Bradford VTS guidance and HEE guidance, effective reflections follow a structured approach:

1
What happened? (Description)

Brief description of the case or event. Keep it concise - focus on what's relevant. Include key facts: patient demographics, presenting complaint, your actions.

Example: "45-year-old man presented with central chest pain. I took a history, performed an ECG, and arranged urgent referral to A&E."
2
What did you think/feel? (Feelings)

Your initial thoughts, concerns, or feelings. This shows self-awareness and emotional intelligence. Be honest about uncertainty, anxiety, or confidence.

Example: "I felt anxious about missing a cardiac event. I was confident in my history-taking but uncertain about whether to refer immediately or investigate first."
3
What did you learn? (Evaluation & Analysis)

The key learning points. What went well? What could have been better? What does the evidence/guidance say? This is where you link to competencies.

Example: "I learned that NICE guidance recommends immediate referral for suspected ACS. I should have referred sooner rather than doing an ECG first. This links to Clinical Knowledge (guidelines), Patient Safety (appropriate urgency), and Communication (explaining the need for urgent referral)."
4
How will this change your practice? (Action Plan)

Specific actions you'll take. Show application of learning. Be concrete and measurable. This demonstrates professional development.

Example: "I will review NICE guidance on chest pain management. For future patients with cardiac-sounding chest pain, I will refer immediately without delay. I will discuss this case with my supervisor to consolidate my learning."

üí° Alternative Reflection Models

You can also use other reflection models. Here are three popular frameworks:

üîÑ Gibbs Reflective Cycle

A six-stage cycle that guides you through reflection systematically:

1
Description

What happened? Describe the event or situation objectively.

2
Feelings

What were you thinking and feeling? Your emotional response and initial thoughts.

3
Evaluation

What was good and bad about the experience? What went well? What didn't?

4
Analysis

What sense can you make of the situation? Link to theory, evidence, or competencies. This is where you demonstrate learning.

5
Conclusion

What else could you have done? What have you learned? Summarize key insights.

6
Action Plan

If it arose again, what would you do? Specific, concrete steps for future practice.

When to use: Complex cases requiring deep analysis, when you want a structured approach to reflection, teaching scenarios.

‚ùì Rolfe et al. Framework (What? So What? Now What?)

A simple three-stage model that encourages critical thinking:

1
What?

What happened? Describe the situation objectively. What did you do? What did others do? What was the outcome?

Example: "A 45-year-old man presented with chest pain. I took a history, performed an ECG, and arranged urgent referral."
2
So What?

What does this mean? Why does it matter? What did you learn? How does it relate to theory, evidence, or competencies? What are the implications?

Example: "I learned that NICE guidance recommends immediate referral for suspected ACS. This links to Clinical Knowledge (guidelines), Patient Safety (appropriate urgency), and Communication (explaining referral)."
3
Now What?

What will you do differently? How will this change your practice? What are your next steps? Be specific and actionable.

Example: "For future patients with cardiac-sounding chest pain, I will refer immediately without delay. I will review NICE guidance and discuss this case with my supervisor."
When to use: Quick reflections, when you want a simple structure, regular portfolio entries, straightforward learning moments.

üí≠ Borton's Framework

A minimalist three-question approach, similar to Rolfe but with slightly different emphasis:

1
What?

What happened? A factual description of the event or experience.

2
So What?

What is the significance? What does this mean for you, the patient, or your practice? What insights have you gained?

3
Now What?

What are you going to do about it? What actions will you take? How will you apply this learning?

When to use: Simple, straightforward reflections, when you want a quick structure, regular practice reflections.

The key is to move beyond description to analysis and action - showing genuine learning and professional development. Choose the model that works best for you, or combine elements from different models.

üìù Complete Example Reflection

Here's a full example following the structure, with three competency links:

Case: Managing Anxiety in a Young Professional

1. What happened? (Description)

A 28-year-old software developer presented with anxiety and panic attacks. She described work stress, difficulty sleeping, and physical symptoms (palpitations, breathlessness). I took a full history including work situation, social support, and previous mental health. I performed a brief mental state examination and discussed management options including self-help, talking therapies, and medication.

2. What did you think/feel? (Feelings)

I felt confident in recognizing anxiety but uncertain about the best initial management approach. I was concerned about medicalizing a normal stress response but also worried about missing something more serious. I felt empathetic towards her situation but also aware of time pressure in a busy clinic.

3. What did you learn? (Evaluation & Analysis)

I learned that NICE guidance recommends stepped care for anxiety - starting with self-help and low-intensity psychological interventions before medication. I realized I was too quick to consider medication when talking therapies might be more appropriate. This case links to:

  • Clinical Knowledge & Skills: Understanding NICE guidance on anxiety management, stepped care approach
  • Communication & Partnership: Exploring patient's ideas, concerns, expectations about anxiety and treatment options
  • Practising Holistically: Considering work stress, social factors, and impact on daily life, not just symptoms

4. How will this change your practice? (Action Plan)

I will review NICE guidance on anxiety management (CG113) to ensure I'm following stepped care appropriately. For future patients with anxiety, I will: (1) Always explore self-help resources and low-intensity interventions first, (2) Discuss patient preferences for treatment modality before prescribing, (3) Consider work and social factors as part of holistic assessment. I will discuss this case with my supervisor to consolidate my learning about anxiety management in primary care.

‚ùå Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå
Too descriptive

Focusing only on what happened without reflection, analysis, or learning. This is a case report, not a reflection.

‚ùå
Single competency link

Only linking to one competency - aim for three to show holistic thinking and integration of skills.

‚ùå
No learning demonstrated

Not showing what you learned, what went well, what could be improved, or how you'll change your practice.

‚ùå
Too generic

Using vague statements like "I will improve my communication" without specific examples or concrete actions.

‚ùå
Avoiding negative experiences

Only reflecting on positive cases. Mistakes and challenges provide the best learning opportunities.

‚ùå
No action plan

Failing to specify how you will change your practice. Reflection without action is incomplete.

‚úÖ Top Tips for Effective Reflections

‚è∞
Write Soon After

Write while details are fresh. According to GMC guidance, timely reflection is more meaningful.

üíØ
Be Honest

Be honest about challenges, mistakes, and uncertainty. This shows genuine self-awareness and growth.

üìà
Show Progression

Demonstrate how you're developing over time. Compare early reflections with later ones to show growth.

üîó
Link Explicitly

Explicitly state which competencies you're linking to. Don't make the reader guess.

üéØ
Be Specific

Include specific examples, concrete actions, and measurable outcomes. Avoid vague statements.

üìù
Keep Focused

One main learning point per reflection. Don't try to cover everything in one entry.

üîó Common Competency Links

Here are common competency combinations that work well together:

Clinical + Communication + Safety

Perfect for cases involving diagnosis, explanation, and risk management.

Example: Chest pain case - clinical knowledge (diagnosis), communication (explaining risk), safety (appropriate referral)

Holistic + Communication + Partnership

Ideal for complex cases requiring understanding of patient context.

Example: Anxiety case - holistic (work/social factors), communication (exploring concerns), partnership (shared decision-making)

Clinical + Ethics + Quality

Useful for cases involving capacity, consent, or ethical dilemmas.

Example: Patient refusing treatment - clinical (treatment options), ethics (capacity/consent), quality (documentation)
