Consultation Models

Structured frameworks for effective consultations

Consultation models provide structured frameworks to ensure comprehensive, patient-centred consultations. Learn when and how to use each model.

üìã Calgary-Cambridge Model

The most widely used consultation model in UK general practice

When to use: Most consultations, especially complex cases requiring comprehensive assessment
1
Initiating the session

Establish rapport, identify reason for attendance

Example: "Hello, I'm Dr. Smith. What brings you in today?" - Open question, maintain eye contact, show interest.
2
Gathering information

Explore patient's perspective, biomedical perspective, background information

Example: "How has this been affecting you?" (patient perspective) ‚Üí "When did the pain start?" (biomedical) ‚Üí "Any family history?" (background)
3
Physical examination

If required, explain and gain consent

Example: "I'd like to examine your chest to listen to your breathing. Is that okay? I'll need you to remove your top."
4
Explanation and planning

Share information, reach shared understanding, involve patient in decisions

Example: "Based on what you've told me, this sounds like asthma. We have a few options: inhaler, lifestyle changes, or both. What do you think would work best for you?"
5
Closing the session

Summarize, safety net, arrange follow-up

Example: "So we've agreed on the inhaler. If your breathing gets worse or you're not better in a week, come back. I'll see you in 2 weeks to check how you're getting on."

‚úÖ Pendleton's 7 Tasks

A task-based approach to consultations

When to use: Teaching, structured assessments, complex cases requiring systematic approach
  • 1 Define the reason for attendance

    Example: "So you've come in about the chest pain you've been having?"

  • 2 Consider other problems

    Example: "Is there anything else you'd like to discuss today?"

  • 3 Choose an appropriate action

    Example: "Given your symptoms, I think we should do an ECG and some blood tests."

  • 4 Achieve a shared understanding

    Example: "So we both agree this is likely anxiety-related chest pain?"

  • 5 Involve the patient in management

    Example: "Would you prefer to try breathing exercises first, or would you like to consider medication?"

  • 6 Use time and resources appropriately

    Example: "I think we can manage this here without needing a referral at this stage."

  • 7 Establish or maintain relationship

    Example: "I'm here if you need to talk about this again. How are you feeling about everything we've discussed?"

‚ö° Neighbour's Inner Consultation

Five checkpoints for effective consultations

When to use: Quick consultations, time-pressured situations, when you need a simple framework
1
Connecting

Establish rapport and understand the patient's agenda

Example: "Hi, how can I help you today?" - Simple, open, welcoming.
2
Summarizing

Check understanding before moving forward

Example: "So you've had this cough for 3 weeks, it's worse at night, and you're worried it might be something serious?"
3
Handing over

Share decision-making and management

Example: "I think this is likely a post-viral cough. We could try a simple cough medicine, or wait another week. What would you prefer?"
4
Safety netting

What to do if things don't improve

Example: "If it's not better in a week, or if you develop a fever or breathlessness, come back or call 111."
5
Housekeeping

Manage your own feelings and maintain boundaries

Example: Reflect after consultation: "Did I handle that well? Am I feeling frustrated? Do I need to debrief with someone?"

üîÑ Byrne & Long Model

Doctor-centred vs patient-centred approach

When to use: Understanding consultation styles, teaching communication skills, reflecting on your approach

‚ùå Doctor-Centred

  • Doctor controls the consultation
  • Closed questions dominate
  • Biomedical focus
  • Doctor makes decisions
  • Less patient involvement
Example: "Where does it hurt? When did it start? Take this medication twice daily."

‚úÖ Patient-Centred

  • Patient's agenda is explored
  • Open questions used
  • Psychosocial factors considered
  • Shared decision-making
  • Patient actively involved
Example: "How has this been affecting you? What are you most worried about? What would you like to do about it?"
üí° Key Learning: Most effective consultations blend both approaches - patient-centred for understanding, doctor-centred for efficiency when appropriate.

üéØ Stott & Davis Model

Four areas to address in every consultation

When to use: Ensuring comprehensive consultations, teaching, checklist for complex cases
A

Management of presenting problem

Address the reason for attendance

Example: Diagnose and treat the chest pain, prescribe appropriate medication
B

Modification of help-seeking behaviour

Address inappropriate attendance patterns

Example: "For minor issues like this, you could try the pharmacy first next time"
C

Management of continuing problems

Address ongoing chronic conditions

Example: Review diabetes control, check blood pressure, adjust medications
D

Opportunistic health promotion

Use the consultation for prevention

Example: "While you're here, have you thought about stopping smoking? I can help with that."

üí° HELPER Mnemonic

A simple mnemonic for consultation structure

When to use: Quick reference, teaching, memory aid for consultation structure
H
History

Take a comprehensive history

Example: Presenting complaint, history of presenting complaint, systems review
E
Examination

Perform relevant physical examination

Example: Cardiovascular exam for chest pain, respiratory exam for cough
L
Likely diagnosis

Formulate differential diagnoses

Example: "This sounds like anxiety-related chest pain, but we should rule out cardiac causes"
P
Plan

Develop management plan

Example: Prescribe medication, arrange investigations, refer if needed
E
Explanation

Explain diagnosis and plan to patient

Example: "Based on what you've told me, this is likely anxiety. Here's what we can do..."
R
Review

Arrange follow-up and safety netting

Example: "Come back in 2 weeks, or sooner if things get worse"

üìö Consultation Guides

Practical guides for managing challenging consultation scenarios in general practice.

üò† Managing Angry Patients

Key Strategies

Active Listening

Acknowledge frustration/anger, don't defend your stance, lean more into how you can help so that you can diffuse their anger.

Stay Calm and Professional

Appreciate that it can be frustrating, offer to work 'together'.

Empathetic Responses

Don't dismiss emotions, mirror this in your consult: 'I can see that you are XYZ, I'm here to help and support you through this'.

Apologise, When Necessary

If simple and would appease the patient, offering an apology can help them feel heard. 'I'm sorry XYZ happened, let's see if we can fix that, and I will feed that back to the team'.

Clarify Misunderstandings

Explain things clearly, acknowledge that it can be hard when a lot of new information is given to you, offer leaflets, videos, or to talk through instructions again.

Offer Solutions

If someone says they can't afford something or they don't like a management option, try to find ways to work together, and offer further solutions - e.g. 'I don't like physiotherapy, it causes me pain' - 'I appreciate that, I think physiotherapy can be quite challenging, could we talk about changing the exercises to make them more accommodating, or taking some painkillers before your sessions to make them easier'.

Set Boundaries

'I want this!' It is not good practice to give into patient demands, especially if they go against guidelines etc. Instead frame this with helpful suggestions that state your boundaries - 'I want to make the best decision for your health, let's discuss these available options, and try to make a plan together'.

CALM-CARE Framework

Letter What it Stands For Actions / How to Apply
C Connect / Compassion Start by recognising the patient's feelings. Show empathy: "I can see you're upset. I understand this situation is frustrating."
A Acknowledge & Ask Acknowledge what they're saying ("You have every right to be concerned") and ask clarifying questions so you understand exactly what's upsetting them.
L Listen actively Let them talk without interruption. Use body language, nodding, paraphrasing to show you're listening.
M Manage tone / Mood Stay calm, keep voice low and steady, avoid defensive or confrontational language. Model the behaviour you'd like.
C Clarify expectations Explore what they're hoping for ("What outcome would you like?"). Ensure both of you understand what can and cannot be done.
A Answer / Apologise if needed Give information honestly. If there was an error or misunderstanding, apologise sincerely. Provide realistic solutions.
R Resolve / Reassure Work with them to find next steps. Reassure them of your commitment to help. Be clear on what will happen and when.
E Evaluate & follow up After the meeting, reflect: Did the patient feel heard? Is further action needed? Schedule a follow-up or check-in if relevant.

üíî Breaking Bad News

Preparation

  • Review notes
  • Familiarise yourself with specifics of the diagnosis
  • Think about common FAQs
  • Think about patient journey - prognosis, treatment, what happens next

Setting the Environment

  • Private, quiet, and comfortable environment
  • Relinquishing bleep or 'duty' expectations where you limit distractions/interruptions

Effective Communication

  • Medical free jargon, easy to understand vocabulary
  • Good: 'The test results we did are for THIS REASON, they are LOW vs HIGH, what we think this means IS XYZ'
  • Bad: Your HbA1c is raised, this is indicative of diabetes

Delivering News - SPIKES Framework

Warning shot: "I'm sorry that this isn't the news we were hoping for"

Direct but sincere: "One of the things we were worried about was cancer. I'm afraid your results show that this is the case."

Pacing and chunking: Deliver information in small, digestible pieces. Check understanding frequently.

Information and Planning

"I want to take the time to explain the diagnosis, some next steps, and what your patient journey may look like. I will check your understanding throughout the conversation, but please let me know at any point if you are not following and stop me if you have any questions."

Responding to Emotions

"I know this is difficult to take in and you must be feeling a lot of different emotions."

Support and Resources

  • There are support groups
  • I can send you more information
  • Should we follow up with an opportunity to ask questions when you have had more time to process things?
  • Can I ask that you see me sooner if there is anything worrying (system specific)

Self Care for Health Professionals

  • Don't burnout
  • Reflect on these conversations, talk about them and write them up if needed

üìã Multiple Presentations

Why Multiple Presentations?

  • Digital triage
  • Limited appointments
  • Accumulating and storing problems - some acute, some chronic etc
  • Complex concerns
  • What is the ICE/or change that prompted the visit?

Risks and Challenges

  • Missing critical diagnosis
  • Being misdiagnosed due to limited time
  • Burnout on the side of the GP - having to fit multiple complaints into a single short consultation
  • Balancing patient satisfaction and safety

Prioritisation

Sharing concerns:

  • "Please tell me about all your concerns, starting with the one you feel is most urgent"
  • "I want to make sure we address what feels more important to begin with, could you tell me about the main issues troubling you"
  • "If I had a magic wand, and could fix one problem right now, which one would it be"

Clarifying and summarising:

  • "To summarise, your main concerns are X,Y,Z, is that correct"
  • "You mentioned a few things, could we start with the most pressing issue"

Prioritising based on severity:

  • "Given the symptoms you described, I feel X might be the most urgent, could we address that first if that's okay with you"
  • "We need to address your most serious concern first, which seems to be X, let's begin with that"

Time Management and Expectations

Thoroughness vs efficiency:

"We have limited time today, and I want to give each one its proper time and attention. Could we address the most pressing issue and make follow up plans for the rest?"

Scheduling follow up appointments:

  • "I would like to schedule a follow up appointment to give your other concerns that attention they deserve"
  • "Could we make another appointment to discuss X, so we can focus on this in depth"

Managing expectations:

  • "I know coming back isn't always convenient, and you may have wanted answers to everything today, but I think it's really important we don't rush your concerns, and be realistic about what we can achieve today"
  • "The goal of today is to make a start, and we can follow up"

Closing the Consultation

"Today we focused on X, and we have a plan in place, we will address Y and Z in another visit."

Ensure patient understanding and agreement. Offer reassurance.

üë• Staff Who Are Patients

Conflict of interest: Avoid providing medical care or prescribing to yourself, or anyone with whom you have a close personal relationship.

Setting the Scene

  • Discourage staff from registering as patients
  • Staff who are patients will always subvert the normal dr-pt relationship and place the dr in difficult positions i.e. in very sensitive matters, or when managing expectations

Setting the Consultation Context

  • "I do want to remind you that our consultation here is strictly confidential and separate from our workplace relationship"
  • "As your GP in this consultation, my focus is solely on your health and wellbeing"

Maintaining Professionalism

  • "My advice will be based on what's best for your health and wellbeing"
  • "We have clear policies about your privacy"
  • "I would encourage you to utilize our formal complaints procedure for workplace issues, as that falls outside my scope as your GP"

Guiding to Appropriate Resources

  • "If this is having an impact on workplace, then it might be worth speaking with management, HR, or an external means of support"
  • "There are resources for workplace issues, which I can direct you to"

Closing the Consultation

"What we discuss does not affect our professional relationship at work."

üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüëß Consult with Relatives and Third Party Consultations

Introduction

Confidentiality and consent:

  • Cannot disclose information without the patient's consent, unless required by LAW or PUBLIC INTEREST
  • Adults - EXPLICIT consent needed for you to share information
  • Children - ASSUMED/BEST INTEREST consent, but try to maintain privacy and autonomy where possible

Data protection: DPA 2018 + GDPR - lawfully, fairly, transparently

Practical Considerations

  • Establish purpose of consultation: Understand relationship - who is calling, nature of relationship, dynamics
  • Information sharing: Minimum amount of necessary information shared where possible
  • Documentation: Good comprehensive documentation about who was spoken to, what was discussed, and decisions made

Power of Attorney

What it is: Legal document that allows an individual (one or more) to make decisions on a person's behalf for:

  1. Health and welfare
  2. Property and financial affairs

When is it valid: When the donor (the person giving power) has mental capacity when creating it, the forms are filled out correctly and signed in the proper order by the donor, attorney(s), and certificate provider, and is subsequently registered with the Office of the Public Guardian (OPG)

What this means for us as GPs:

  • Decision making: Yes in H&W, but always think about patient's best interest
  • Confidentiality: Can access patient's medical information (if justified)
  • Verification: You should verify the LPA (existence + validity)
  • Ethical considerations: Try to value autonomy, and best interests always

Understanding Context and Confirming Consent

  • "Could you please clarify, the patient's name, DOB"
  • "Thank you, could I please get your name, and your relationship to them"
  • "Thank you, could I please ask if they have consented to you speaking with me, or you have a power of attorney for health"
  • "Could I ask why they were not able to attend today"

Setting Expectations

  • "While I value the information you provide, I just want you to be aware that our ability to make certain decisions may be limited without the X's direct input. It would be good if we focused on gathering information today and make follow up plans that involve X"
  • "I appreciate your concerns today, my ability to make decisions without speaking to X might be limited"

Setting Boundaries

  • Limit information shared on your end
  • Encourage direct patient contact
  • Do not assume consent
  • Do not share information freely
  • Do not disregard patient autonomy

Closing the Conversation (Respectfully)

"Thank you for your time, let's make X aware of what we discussed and could you please let them know that they can contact me directly for further concerns."

üéØ Demanding Patients

Increasing number of demands from patients - for investigation, treatments etc.

Key Strategies

Active Listening

"I can see this very important to you, could you tell me more so I can better understand how to help"

Stay Calm and Professional

"I understand the frustration/upset, could we try and find a solution together, I'm here to help"

Set Clear Boundaries

Explain what can be achieved from the conversation, time constraints, treatment limitations. Explain why certain requests can be fulfilled. "I know you came here with some specific suggestions in mind, I have to balance those with medical guidelines and my scope of practice, unfortunately there are limitations to what we can prescribe or what scans we can request but I'm hopeful we can agree on a management plan together"

Manage Expectations

"You may expect this problem to go away in X amount of time, but I want to explain this may last until X time, in the meantime look out for the following as that may mean you need to see me sooner or consider changing our management"

Empathise but be Assertive

"I appreciate your concerns, my medical advice is about doing what's going to be most effective and best for your health"

Key to Demanding Patients

  • Open ended questioning: Explore their concerns fully
  • Educating the patient: "Let me explain why this is the recommended approach" "If we took the time to understand your treatment, I think this could ease some of your concerns"
  • Involve them in decision making: "Let's look at all the options together, I appreciate your input, how do you feel about the options we're considering"
  • Follow up plans: "I suggest we follow up to assess, progress, and address any ongoing concerns"

ü©π Chronic Pain

Pain is an unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with, or resembling that associated with, actual or potential tissue damage.

Introduction

  • Subjectivity: Pain is personal and varies between individuals
  • Sensory and emotional components: Both physical sensation and emotional response
  • Potential damage: May not always indicate ongoing tissue damage
  • Variability: Pain experience can change over time

The Purpose of Pain

  • Alerts us to danger
  • Promotes healing
  • Indicates a health issue
  • Learning and adaptation
  • Regulatory function

Opening the Consult

  • Acknowledge time constraints
  • Understanding the pain's effects
  • Consultation goals

Safety and Red Flags

  • Make sure that there are no red flags
  • Enquire about functional impact, ability to safely examine

Effective Communication to Assure the Patient

Questions to ask:

  • What do you think is going on?
  • How do you think we can help?
  • What have others told you about what the pain means?

You should respond:

  • "I have asked about and examined for anything serious or dangerous, I'm reassured that this isn't the case, but I understand the pain is affecting you and worrying you, so should we try to focus on how we can address that"
  • "Pain when moving doesn't mean you are doing more harm"

Explain What's Going On

  • Pain is not always an indication that something bad is happening
  • Think of fire alarms that go off without fire, in this same way, our responses can become too sensitive or faulty i.e. like allergies, our body is responding more than it should
  • Sometimes this causes the muscles to spasm, as the body is trying to protect the spine
  • This does not mean you cannot use the muscle, in fact, gentle exercises are a good way to start to increase blood flow, and relax the muscle, which overtime will help with the spasm and the pain

Implementing Strategies

  • Pain is likely chronic, the goal is about symptom control and QOL
  • The concept of pain management - not pain reduction
  • Address poor health beliefs and behaviours - i.e. cannot exercise, osteopathy (manual manipulation), solicitous families/benefits etc.

Alternative Treatment Options

  • Pain clinic, MDT, physiotherapy
  • "Referring is about trying to find solutions that improve your QOL, but is unlikely to get rid of pain completely"
  • "Their output will only be as effective as your input, is there anything that might limit you from engaging"

Rethinking Medication Efficacy

"Our body builds up tolerance overtime, it's why we often need more of something for the same overall effect, the best thing we can do, is to reduce the doses of the medication, so your body can become more sensitive to them again."

Counter Arguments by the Patient

Things patients say:

  • The painkillers are the only things that work
  • I missed a dose and the pain was really bad

Things to say to patients:

  • Our body can have withdrawal reactions from medication, it doesn't mean the painkillers are working, it means we've become a little too reliant on them
  • If we reduce the painkillers slowly, this may cause some pain for a few days, but this is whilst your body adjusts to the lower doses
  • "We know that pain eventually settles and medications can have long term side effects, it's important that we try to wean the doses down"

üòê Blas√© Patients

Indifferent patients could mean deeper concerns they are trying to dismiss, or past negative health experiences.

Initial Approach

"I've noticed that discussing your health issues doesn't seem easy for you. Can you tell me more about your experience with healthcare in the past?"

Building Rapport

  • Empathetic engagement: Show understanding and non-judgmental approach
  • Non-confrontational open-ended questions: Allow them to express themselves
  • Active listening with validation: Acknowledge their feelings and experiences

Challenging and Modifying Health Beliefs

  • Identifying misconceptions: Gently explore their understanding
  • Motivational interviewing: Help them find their own motivation
  • Cognitive reframing: Help them see things from a different perspective

Communication Techniques

  • Clarity and relevance: Use simple, clear language
  • Empathy and tone: Maintain warm, understanding tone
  • Feedback loop: Check understanding regularly

Building a Therapeutic Relationship

  • Trust and safety: Create a safe, non-judgmental space
  • Consistency in care: "I would like you to follow up with me, so that you feel more consistency in your care"
  • Personalisation of care: "I recall you saying this was important to you, can I..."

Handling Resistance

  • Gradual engagement: Don't push too hard, build trust gradually
  • Use of analogies: Try to simplify complex medical information
  • Empathetic confrontation: "I understand you are hesitant about medication, was there anything I can explain that might help, would you be open to starting on a low dose, and we can monitor you closely to see how you feel" "I want us to agree on a management that is safe, but that you will also be happy with, could we agree on this middle ground"

Empowering the Patient

  • Highlight control and autonomy: Highlight how engaging in their health can empower them. "There are some lifestyle changes we can recommend, I can explain why they would be a really important part of your care"
  • Collaborative goal setting: "What are some of the goals you had, what feels achievable, where can we start in order to make that more possible"
  • Reinforcement of progress: Progress takes time, but slow and small is very good. "Can I signpost you to some resources or additional support that might help"

Cultural Competence

  • Understanding cultural health beliefs: Respect cultural backgrounds
  • Incorporating cultural practices: Try to embed this into management. "Are there any cultural practices that you might want me to be aware of, or try to involve in your management"
  • Cultural sensitivity in communication: Try to be sensitive. "Are you comfortable speaking in English, would you prefer if we got an interpreter for our consultation"

Dealing with Complexity

  • Holistic assessment: Explore psychosocial concerns. "Sometimes how we feel emotionally can impact our physical health, have you noticed any changes in your mood or stress levels"
  • Coordination of care: MDT involvement, means you don't have to do all the work. "I think a team approach would be really beneficial for your care, would you be open to referrals and input from other members of the team"
  • Flexibility in approach: "Try different things, schedule regular check ins, be willing to adjust"

üò∞ Health Anxiety

Definition: Excessive worry about having a serious illness despite medical reassurance. Health anxiety can include a fear of having a serious illness or the belief that you already have a serious illness.

Key Point: We all worry about our health from time to time, but health worries become a problem when they get in the way of normal life even if there is no clear evidence that anything is seriously wrong.

Challenges: Patients often do not recognise their anxiety as a health issue, complicating the consultation process. They may seek repeated reassurance from health professionals.

Recognising Health Anxiety

Common presentations include:

  • Fear that a physical symptom is due to a serious illness
  • Worrying about health to the point that it affects how they live their life
  • Physical anxiety symptoms such as increased heart rate or stomach upset
  • Seeking reassurance from friends, family and health professionals
  • Checking or scanning body for symptoms
  • Avoiding things that remind them of illness or that may make them ill
  • Fear that serious illness could not be cured by modern medicine
  • Fear that being unwell would mean a loss of dignity and quality of life

Understanding Triggers

Common triggers for health anxiety include:

Stress

When we're already stressed or anxious, we instinctively become more aware of possible threats which can lead us to focus on potential threats to our health.

Extra Responsibility

When we become responsible for something or someone other than ourselves (e.g. having your first child), our anxiety can focus on what would happen if something were to happen to us.

Exposure to Serious Illness

The more we're exposed to illness or death (through someone we know, TV, radio), the more we can overestimate the likelihood of ourselves becoming ill.

Experiencing Serious Illness or Unexplained Symptoms

Health anxiety is not only for people who are 'healthy'. Sometimes people with existing or undiagnosed illnesses can experience this too. The main difficulty is the extent to which they are pre-occupied with worry and what they do to try and manage these worries.

The Vicious Cycle of Health Anxiety

Health anxiety creates a cycle:

  1. Trigger: Physical sensation or health-related thought
  2. Misinterpretation: "This must be something serious"
  3. Anxiety: Physical symptoms of anxiety (increased heart rate, stomach upset)
  4. Safety Behaviours: Checking body, seeking reassurance, avoiding triggers
  5. Short-term relief: But anxiety returns, maintaining the cycle

Key insight: When we experience health anxiety, we tend to spend a lot of time focusing on particular parts of our body. When we're so focused on one area, we're going to notice every single sensation or change ‚Äì most of which will be completely normal sensations that your body makes every day.

Consultation Approach

1. Normalise and Validate

"Many people can experience concerns about their health that can amplify their stress. It's a common issue, and can be addressed effectively. I can see this is really worrying you."

2. Explain Misinterpretation of Symptoms

Help them understand how focusing on body sensations can amplify normal sensations:

"When we spend too much time focusing on one body part, we will naturally notice changes and new sensations which are normal. If your health anxiety means you focus on one part of your body, you might just be noticing normal changes as opposed to problem changes that otherwise you would have not picked up on."

3. Identify Safety Behaviours

Explore what they do to manage their anxiety:

  • Going to the GP surgery very often for reassurance (or avoiding going at all)
  • Googling symptoms
  • Avoiding medical programmes or the news (or watching them to try and find those with a positive outcome)
  • Reassurance seeking from friends and family
  • Checking body for changes and sensations

"Safety behaviours can appear helpful in the short term, but they also maintain our anxiety because we believe that the reason we're able to get by is because of the safety behaviour. We miss out on the opportunity to learn that there was no threat or that we're able to cope on our own."

4. Explain the Anxiety Response

"There is a release of adrenaline when we become anxious which can impact our physical responses. But we also know that we can only produce so much of this. The physical symptoms will eventually reduce, once the adrenaline does. If we face an anxiety-provoking situation and stay in it, anxiety goes up quickly, but with time (usually after approximately twenty minutes) will go down again."

5. Introduce Graded Exposure

Explain that overcoming anxiety involves actively letting go of safety behaviours and confronting avoided situations gradually:

The Four Rules of Graded Exposure:

  1. Graded: Start with situations that are less anxiety-provoking and work up to more difficult situations
  2. Prolonged: Stay in that situation until your anxiety has reduced by at least 50%
  3. Repeated: Repeat each exposure activity until your anxiety rating has reduced by 50% before moving to the next step
  4. Without distraction: Be aware of safety behaviours and avoid them. Truly feel the anxiety to learn that anxiety levels will reduce
6. Set Boundaries Around Reassurance

"I understand you're worried, but repeatedly checking or seeking reassurance actually maintains the anxiety. Instead, let's work on strategies to manage the worry itself. We can agree on a plan for when you should come back, but let's try to reduce the checking and reassurance-seeking behaviours."

7. Offer Structured Support

Consider referral to:

  • IAPT/Talking Therapies: For CBT-based approaches to health anxiety
  • Self-help resources: Recommend workbooks on health anxiety (e.g., Centre for Clinical Interventions, Berkshire Healthcare NHS resources)
  • Grounding techniques: Help redirect attention away from body focus

Practical Tips for the Consultation

‚úÖ Do
  • Validate their concerns without reinforcing the anxiety
  • Explain how anxiety can cause physical symptoms
  • Help them understand the cycle of health anxiety
  • Set clear boundaries around repeated consultations
  • Offer structured follow-up rather than ad-hoc reassurance
  • Refer to appropriate psychological support
‚ùå Avoid
  • Dismissing their concerns as "just anxiety"
  • Ordering unnecessary investigations for reassurance
  • Providing repeated reassurance without addressing the underlying anxiety
  • Getting frustrated with repeated attendances
  • Missing genuine red flags (always do appropriate clinical assessment)
